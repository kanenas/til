## How many characters should the optimal length of ALT & TITLE for images be for SEO?  
The short answer: **ALT text should be around 7–12 words (approx. 80–125 characters). TITLE attributes don't have a strict "SEO" character limit, but functionally, you should keep them under 75–80 characters for usability.**

Focus on **context and utility**. Here is the tactical breakdown of why these limits matter and how to execute them.

### 1. ALT Text: The Accessibility & Ranking Standard

**Optimal Range: 80–125 Characters**
**Hard Limit: 125 Characters**

**The Strategy:**
While Google officially states they look at the content of the ALT attribute, they do not publish a strict character limit. However, we follow the **125-character rule** based on assistive technology limitations.

- **Screen Readers:** Most screen readers (used by visually impaired users) cut off ALT text at around 125 characters. If you exceed this, the user experiences a jarring, incomplete audio description. Since accessibility (ADA/WCAG) is a ranking factor and a legal requirement, we optimize for the user experience first.
- **Keyword Relevance:** This limit forces you to be concise. It prevents "keyword stuffing," which Google’s Penguin algorithm and its SpamBrain system are exceptionally good at detecting in images.
- **The "Comic Sans" Rule:** If your ALT text is so long it would look ridiculous in a comic strip speech bubble (describing the image), it’s too long.

**Execution:**
- **Do:** "Nordic skier carving through deep powder snow in a backcountry forest at sunset."
- **Don't:** "Skier skiing down a ski slope in the mountains with trees and snow and sky." (Too vague) or "Cheap skis for sale Norway cheap ski holidays ski deals 2024." (Keyword stuffing).

### 2. TITLE Attribute: The UX Signal (Not a Ranking Factor)

**Optimal Range: 0–75 Characters**
**Hard Limit: None, but 80 is the UX ceiling.**

**The Strategy:**
Let’s be clear: The image `title` attribute has **negligible to zero impact on search rankings**.
We use the TITLE attribute exclusively for **User Experience (UX)** . It functions as a tooltip that appears when a user hovers over an image.

- **The Hover Test:** If a user hovers over an image and a tooltip pops up that is cut off or runs off the screen, it looks unprofessional. Most browsers display tooltips as a single line of text. While they can wrap, it’s rare and ugly.
- **When to use it:** You do not need a TITLE attribute on every image. Only use it if the hover text provides additional, non-critical information that aids the user. Often, if your ALT text is good, the TITLE is redundant.

**Execution:**
- If your ALT is "Infographic showing 2024 SEO trends," your TITLE could be "Click to enlarge the 2024 trends summary."
- If you have nothing useful to add, **leave the TITLE attribute blank.** A blank attribute is better than a redundant or auto-generated one.

### The "SEO Manager" Cheat Sheet

Here is how I want to think about it:

1.  **ALT Text (125 chars):** Describe the image specifically as if you are on the phone telling someone what is happening. Include the primary keyword *if it fits naturally* and is relevant to the page context. If the keyword doesn't fit, don't force it.
2.  **TITLE Attribute (75 chars):** Provide additional context or a call-to-action for the user hovering. If you have nothing to say, omit it.
3.  **The File Name (50 chars):** Don't forget this! Before Google reads the ALT, it reads the file name. Use hyphens, be descriptive, and include the primary keyword here. (e.g., `nordic-skier-sunset-backcountry.jpg`).

**Final Verdict:** Stop obsessing over the TITLE tag length. Put your energy into writing concise, descriptive **ALT text under 125 characters** that serves the user and naturally incorporates the target keyword. That is where the ROI is.
